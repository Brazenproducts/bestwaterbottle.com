How to Clean a Water Bottle (Including the Lid and Straw)

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

Water bottles get gross faster than most people realize. Even if you only fill yours with plain water, a combination of moisture, warmth, backwash, and irregular cleaning creates the perfect conditions for biofilm, mold, and bacteria to thrive. Here's how to clean every part of your bottle properly — including the parts most people skip.

How Often Should You Clean Your Water Bottle?

The short answer: every day. Rinse after each use, wash with soap at least daily if you use it regularly, and do a deep clean once a week. If you're filling it with anything other than water — sports drinks, juice, coffee — wash it immediately after each use. Sugar is fuel for bacteria.

What You'll Need

  • Dish soap
  • Bottle brush (long enough to reach the bottom)
  • Straw cleaning brush (thin, flexible)
  • Small detail brush for lid crevices (an old toothbrush works great)
  • White vinegar or baking soda (for deep cleaning)
  • Warm water

Step-by-Step: Daily Cleaning

  1. Disassemble everything. Remove the lid, straw, mouthpiece, gaskets, and any other removable parts. These can't be cleaned properly when attached.
  2. Rinse the bottle with warm water. Get the initial residue out before adding soap.
  3. Add a drop of dish soap and hot water to the bottle. Use a bottle brush to scrub the interior thoroughly — sides, bottom, and especially the shoulder where it narrows near the mouth.
  4. Clean the lid. Lids have more surface area and crevices than the bottle itself. Scrub around the threads, the pour spout, and any hinge mechanisms. Flip-top lids trap gunk in the hinge. Screw lids trap it in the threads.
  5. Clean the straw. Push a thin straw brush through the straw multiple times. Run water through it after. If the brush won't fit, soak the straw in hot soapy water, then flush with water.
  6. Remove and clean gaskets/silicone seals. These are where mold hides. Pop them out, scrub with a toothbrush, rinse thoroughly, and leave them out to dry before reassembling.
  7. Rinse everything thoroughly — multiple times. Soap residue affects taste.
  8. Air dry completely before capping. Storing a bottle with any moisture inside is how mold starts.

Can You Put Water Bottles in the Dishwasher?

It depends on the bottle. Many stainless steel water bottles — including popular Hydro Flask models — are technically dishwasher safe on the top rack, but the manufacturer often recommends hand washing to preserve the vacuum insulation and exterior finish. Plastic water bottles vary widely; check the bottom for the dishwasher-safe symbol.

Even dishwasher-safe bottles need the straw and gasket removed and hand-cleaned — dishwashers don't get water inside straws effectively.

Deep Cleaning: Weekly or When You Notice Smell

For a thorough clean when daily washing hasn't kept up with buildup:

Option 1: Vinegar Soak

  1. Fill the bottle halfway with a 50/50 mix of white vinegar and water
  2. Soak all parts (lid, straw) in a separate bowl with the same solution
  3. Let everything soak for 30 minutes to 2 hours
  4. Scrub with brushes, rinse extremely thoroughly (vinegar taste lingers if you don't)
  5. Air dry fully

Option 2: Baking Soda Paste

  1. Make a paste of 1–2 tablespoons baking soda and enough water to form a paste
  2. Apply to the interior and let sit for 15–20 minutes
  3. Scrub with bottle brush, rinse thoroughly
  4. Good for neutralizing odors without leaving a taste residue

Option 3: Bottle Cleaning Tablets

Products like Hydro Flask's Bottle Bright tablets are designed exactly for this. Drop one in with water, wait, rinse. Convenient and effective.

Parts People Always Miss

  • The gasket under the lid: Silicone gaskets are removable in most bottles. They trap moisture and grow mold if never removed and dried.
  • The mouthpiece nozzle: Soft silicone spouts have a tiny hole that harbors bacteria. Use a thin brush or soak in vinegar.
  • The bottom of the bottle interior: The brush needs to reach all the way down. Short sponges leave the bottom untouched.
  • The cap threads: Grime collects in the grooves where the lid screws on. A toothbrush gets in there.

How to Dry It Properly

Upside down on a drying rack is fine for the bottle. Leave the lid off completely. For the straw, stand it upright so air circulates through it. Don't assemble until everything is fully dry — touching to check isn't enough, leave it for at least an hour.

A clean bottle stays odor-free and keeps you healthier. Spending three minutes every day beats a 30-minute deep clean every time you notice the smell.
