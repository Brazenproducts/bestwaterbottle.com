How to Get Rid of That Weird Smell in Your Water Bottle

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

Your water bottle smells. Maybe it's musty and organic, or plasticky and chemical, or it permanently smells like the protein shake you had three months ago. Whatever the odor, it's fixable — and the fix is usually easier than people think.

Why Water Bottles Smell

There are a few different sources, and the cause determines the cure:

  • Biofilm / mold / mildew: A thin layer of bacteria or mold growing on the interior surface, lid, or gaskets. Smells musty, earthy, or just "off." This is the most common cause.
  • Absorbed beverage odors: Coffee, tea, sports drinks, and protein shakes can leave chemical compounds absorbed into plastic or silicone parts that regular soap doesn't fully remove.
  • New plastic smell: Some bottles off-gas from manufacturing — a chemical or plastic smell that dissipates over time.
  • Metallic taste/smell: Usually from stainless steel bottles that haven't been broken in, or from low-quality steel.
  • Silicone smell: Gaskets and lids with silicone seals can develop an odor, especially if not removed and dried regularly.

Method 1: Baking Soda (Best for Mold/Mildew Smell)

Baking soda neutralizes acidic and basic odor compounds and is safe, cheap, and effective.

  1. Add 2 tablespoons of baking soda to the bottle
  2. Fill with warm water, cap, and shake
  3. Leave overnight (or at least 4–6 hours)
  4. Scrub with a bottle brush, rinse thoroughly multiple times
  5. Air dry completely with lid off

For lids and gaskets: make a paste of baking soda and water, apply to the affected parts, let sit, scrub, rinse.

Method 2: White Vinegar (Best for Stubborn Odors and Mold)

White vinegar is mildly acidic and kills many types of bacteria and mold.

  1. Fill the bottle 1/4 full with undiluted white vinegar
  2. Cap it and shake to coat the interior
  3. Let sit for 5 minutes to several hours depending on severity
  4. Pour out and fill with warm soapy water; scrub
  5. Rinse very thoroughly — vinegar taste lingers if you don't rinse enough

Soak lids and gaskets in a bowl of undiluted white vinegar for 30 minutes.

Method 3: Hydrogen Peroxide (Best for Serious Mold)

3% hydrogen peroxide (standard drugstore variety) is an effective disinfectant and deodorizer.

  1. Pour 1/4 cup of 3% hydrogen peroxide into the bottle
  2. Swirl to coat, then add water to fill halfway
  3. Let sit for 30 minutes
  4. Scrub with a bottle brush, then rinse extensively

Don't use bleach inside bottles — it's hard to remove entirely and can be harmful if ingested.

Method 4: Cleaning Tablets (Easiest Option)

Products like Bottle Bright or Hydro Flask's Bottle Brush Cleaning Tablets are specifically designed for water bottles. Drop one tablet in with warm water, wait 15–30 minutes, rinse. Great for maintenance cleaning and breaking down drink residue.

Fixing a New Plastic Smell

New bottles, especially plastic ones, sometimes off-gas chemicals that create a plasticky smell. This is normal and usually temporary. To speed it up:

  1. Fill with a 50/50 mix of water and white vinegar
  2. Let sit overnight
  3. Rinse thoroughly and air dry
  4. Repeat once or twice if needed

If the smell persists strongly after several weeks of regular use, the bottle may be low quality — consider replacing it with something BPA-free and higher grade.

Fixing a Metallic Taste or Smell

New stainless steel bottles often have a faint metallic taste. This usually goes away with a few uses and regular washing. To accelerate it:

  1. Make a diluted white vinegar solution (1 part vinegar to 3 parts water)
  2. Fill the bottle, cap, and let sit for 24 hours
  3. Rinse thoroughly multiple times

If you have a Stanley tumbler or Hydro Flask that's developed a metallic taste after regular use, the interior coating may be compromised — contact the manufacturer.

Fixing Smelly Silicone Parts

Silicone gaskets and mouthpieces are odor traps. The silicone is porous enough to absorb flavors and odors.

  1. Remove all silicone parts
  2. Soak in white vinegar for 30 minutes
  3. Boil them (if not part of an insulated lid) for 5–10 minutes — heat kills mold spores and drives out absorbed odors
  4. Let air dry completely before reassembling

If silicone parts persistently smell bad after multiple attempts, replacement gaskets and mouthpieces are available for most popular brands for a few dollars.

Preventing Future Smells

  • Never cap a wet bottle. This is the #1 cause of mold and mildew. Always leave the lid off until completely dry.
  • Wash same day if you use anything other than water. Sugar drinks and protein shakes need to be cleaned out within hours, not days.
  • Remove gaskets and dry them separately. They're the most-neglected part.
  • Deep clean weekly. Regular washing prevents buildup; vinegar or baking soda weekly keeps odors from establishing.

Most water bottle smells are fixable in an afternoon. If nothing works, the bottle may have visible mold in crevices that can't be reached — at that point, replacement is genuinely the safer choice.
